The Build vs Buy Dilemma in Software Engineering

Every technology leader eventually faces the classic engineering dilemma: should we build this internal tool ourselves, or buy an off-the-shelf SaaS product? While engineering teams often have a bias toward building (the "Not Invented Here" syndrome), the financial reality is usually quite different.

The True Cost of Building

When estimating the cost of building custom software, companies frequently underestimate the ongoing costs. A typical internal tool has three cost phases:

  • Initial Development: The hours required for developers, designers, and product managers to release V1.
  • Maintenance (The Hidden Killer): Server hosting, security patches, library updates, and fixing bugs. Industry standard estimates maintenance at 20% of the initial build cost per year.
  • Opportunity Cost: If your best engineers are building an internal billing system instead of your core product, you are losing potential revenue.

When Should You Buy?

You should almost always buy SaaS when the problem you are solving is not a core differentiator for your business. Examples include HR systems, payroll, basic CRM, or authentication. If a specialized company has spent millions optimizing a tool, paying them $500/month is significantly cheaper than deploying a $150,000/year engineer to reinvent the wheel.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) calculated?
TCO includes all direct and indirect costs over a given period (usually 1 or 3 years). For SaaS, it includes subscription fees and integration costs. For custom builds, it includes developer salaries, server costs, and ongoing maintenance.
When is it better to BUILD instead of BUY?
You should build internally when the feature is a core competitive advantage of your product, when existing SaaS tools have strict data privacy/compliance issues that violate your policies, or when your specific use case is so unique that no off-the-shelf product exists.
